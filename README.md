# CS330-SNHU
## How do I approach designing software?
This project not only deepened my understanding of software design before coding, but also equipped me with a strong skill of “breaking a real-world scene into primitive shapes.” Instead of trying to model everything at once, it was like looking at a photo and asking, “What basic shapes should I combine to make this object?” So, with a coffee mug, I figured out that a cylinder and a torus are sufficient, while for the cutting board, it was just a box lying flat. This method of reduction is very much like what software design requires: dissecting a complex system into the smallest useful components before implementation.
I picked an iterative, milestone-based approach for the design process. A milestone targeted one specific outcome: first add geometry, then interactivity, then textures, and finally lighting, which helped keep the scope in check. Such a method is very similar to agile development, and I have no doubt I will keep using it: outline a crisp yet complete vertical slice of functionality, implement it, and then extend it. The routine of ensuring a working state before adding the next level of complexity is something that I will follow in every future project, whether graphics are involved or not.
## How do I approach developing programs?
One of the main innovations that I incorporated in this project is the use of modular helper functions to separate concerns. For example, instead of repeating the transformation calculations or shader settings for each object, I depended on “SetTransformations(),” “SetShaderTexture(),” “SetShaderColor(),” “SetTextureUVScale(),” and “SetShaderMaterial()” to hide these operations behind single, intuitive function calls. Objects in “RenderScene()” were written as a block of 5 lines each instead of 20. This resulted in dramatically easier debugging and code extending.
Iteration was the very heart of the development process. Any change to the position, size, or texture of an object meant going through the cycles of rebuilding, running the application, comparing the output to the reference image, and modifying. After many iterations, I developed an instinct for how OpenGL coordinate values correspond to screen positions and sizes. My coding style has changed too over the milestones: at the beginning of the project, I used to put objects at coordinates that were whole numbers and hope for the best; however, by the end of the final project I was able to logically think about the height of the board surface, the radius of cylinders, and how rotation can change the origin of meshes. It was the internalization of deliberate spatial reasoning, rather than trial and error, that was the biggest rebirth of my skills in the course.
## How can computer science help me in reaching my goals?
Computational graphics and visualization provided me with hands-on practice in a computer science area that is rarely discussed in introductory courses: the mathematical pipeline from 3D world coordinates to a 2D screen. Grasping how the model, view, and projection matrices combine, understanding that the Phong shading model approximates physical light behavior, and learning how UV coordinates map a 2D image onto a 3D surface gives me a solid foundation that extends beyond just OpenGL. Game engines, scientific visualization tools, AR/VR systems, and even data visualization libraries employ similar concepts. From an educational perspective, this insight will be beneficial for future computer graphics, simulation, and human-computer interaction courses.
In a career context, the abilities I honed in this class, such as structured problem breakdown, using low-level graphics APIs, reading, and writing highly commented C++ code, and handling a multi-file Visual Studio project, are directly linked to job openings in game development, simulation engineering, and interactive media. On a broader scale, creating a visual, tangible artifact from mathematical primitives increased my confidence in dealing with unfamiliar technical fields. Opening the OpenGL documentation, explaining coordinate and space transformations, and fixing rendering errors without using a visual debugger are problem-solving techniques applicable to any engineering position.
